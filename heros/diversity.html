DIVERSITY AND UNIFORMITY

Anatomistâ€™s of the 15th and early 16th century disregarded the importance of analyzing anatomical variability. In 1559, Realdo Colombo, an Italian surgeon, widespread the idea that anatomical anomalies are incredibly rare, and as such, should not be an emphasis of study. Tall, middle-aged men were the primary demographic for anatomical research and dissections. Going against the grain, Andreas Vesalius heavily considered this issue of representation within his book De Humani Corporis Fabrica.

By analyzing bodies from different demographics, Vesalius was able to categorize anatomical diversity among ethnic groups due to their differences in cartilage, bones, and muscles. This categorization may have allowed society a more encompassing perspective on what is considered natural for the human body (Siraisi 62).
